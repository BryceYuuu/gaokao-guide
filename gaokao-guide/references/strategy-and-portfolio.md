# Strategy And Portfolio

Use this file when building candidate pools, low-estimated opportunities, hot-risk alternatives, or final volunteer portfolios.

## Portfolio Layers

Every serious plan should include:

1. 主方案: closest to declared preferences with acceptable safety.
2. 对冲方案: protects against narrow preference, hot-city crowding, hot-major crowding, and weak safety.
3. 机会方案: lower-consensus choices that may improve long-term leverage.
4. 真保底: choices the student would actually attend, not just names that look safe.

## Candidate Discovery

Start wide, then narrow:

- Same province and declared target cities.
- Nearby city clusters and lower-competition cities.
- Industry-featured schools: electric power, transport, petroleum, posts, finance, politics/law, medicine, teacher education, agriculture, water resources, civil aviation.
- High-platform but geographically discounted choices.
- Strong vocational or applied options when weak undergraduate choices have poor basic quality.
- Special plans, directed training, public-funded teacher/medical programs only when obligations are acceptable.

## Low-Estimated Opportunity Rules

Do not call something a “捡漏” unless it has a tolerable basic floor.

Check:

- Basic floor: school quality, city, cost, campus, group lower bound, major acceptability.
- Upward hypothesis: policy, industry, merger, school upgrade, new degree authorization, regional development, or underpriced professional strength.
- Evidence: official or reliable sources, not short-video narratives.
- Failure cost: if the hypothesis fails, can the student still transfer, graduate, work, take exams, or pursue graduate school?
- Repricing: if recent ranks already rose, the opportunity may be gone.

## Overheat Audit

Flag possible peak-buying when:

- The school/major/city rose sharply in recent ranks without matching quality improvement.
- The user is chasing a social-media hot major with vague understanding.
- Professional group lower bound contains unattractive majors masked by one hot major.
- The same employment story has too many candidates and too few quality jobs.

## Family Resource Matching

Treat family background as a practical constraint, not a moral score.

- Strong local system resources: law, medicine, teacher education, finance, civil service, state-owned enterprise, or local industry may have stronger fit.
- Ordinary family with low trial capacity: prefer clearer skills, license paths, public-sector exams, and lower sunk-cost routes.
- High-budget, high-support family: can tolerate longer training, overseas paths, interdisciplinary exploration, or delayed returns.
- First-generation college student: emphasize transparent rules, cost, dorm/campus stability, career service, and exam-oriented upward paths.

## Student Execution Risk

Do not hide execution problems.

| Signal | Risk | Treatment |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Strong rejection of a major | Hanging courses, poor GPA, transfer failure | Mark as high-cost option unless family accepts risk. |
| Weak math but wants CS/AI/electronics | Core-course pressure | Offer adjacent or applied alternatives. |
| Weak chemistry/biology but wants medicine | Long training and heavy science load | Clarify clinician vs medical technology vs nursing vs pharmacy. |
| Wants stability but rejects exams | Conflict | Show civil-service/teacher/medical paths require sustained exams. |
| Wants high income but rejects pressure | Conflict | Show industry volatility and workload. |

## Final Table Balance

Balance depends on province rules and risk preference, but audit these defaults:

- Conservative: fewer reach slots, deeper safety, more acceptable fallback groups.
- Balanced: reach/opportunity slots exist, but safety is not symbolic.
- Aggressive: only acceptable if family understands滑档/调剂/专业下限 risk.

For professional-group provinces, safety is valid only when the whole group or likely admission major range is acceptable.

## Re-Ranking Logic

When users change constraints, re-run the whole ordering instead of patching one row:

1. Reapply hard constraints.
2. Rebuild candidate layers.
3. Update evidence status.
4. Reassign tiers.
5. Rebalance portfolio.
6. Explain what changed and why.
